![1973 BMW 2002 on the flatbed, front view](/images/2002-06.jpg)

Some cars earn their reputation. The BMW 2002 earned its place in automotive history by being exactly what it needed to be at exactly the right moment.

When BMW introduced the 2002 to the US market in 1968, the company was close to irrelevant here. In 1966, BMW's US sales totaled just 1,253 cars (!). What changed things was a simple idea: take a small two-door body and drop in a larger engine. The story I've heard is that some of the engineers at BMW had been driving the 1600, a car that is the predecessor to the 2002. They loved the handling of the independent suspension, but wanted more power than the 1600cc motor could produce. The solution was to stuff their largest M10 engine into their tiny 1600s and the 2002 was born. Sales in the first year alone were 9,637 units, and BMW was suddenly a name people knew. Car and Driver called it something close to impossible: a car that worked for the driving enthusiast and the sensible buyer at the same time. With its inline-four, four-wheel independent suspension, and excellent visibility in all directions, the 2002 stood out sharply among its contemporaries. Light, and fast with such good handling that it was a joy to drive, it laid the foundation for everything BMW would become.

---

By 1973, the car had been on the road for five years and the formula was still working. That summer, a man named Donald R. Campbell bought one. Campbell was stationed at Gibbs Kaserne, a US Army installation in Frankfurt, one of hundreds of facilities across West Germany where roughly 250,000 American troops were based during the Cold War. He bought the car on August 1 from BMW Heiland, a dealership in Gross-Gerau, about twenty kilometers south of the city. Two weeks and 600 miles later, he took it back to Heiland for the first service. They did the 1,000km inspection, installed an antenna, and replaced a rear bumper rubber. It's possible that he had a fender bender. Eight days after that, he shipped it to Houston, Texas.

The title was processed on October 11, 1973. Campbell's address on the American paperwork is a house on the west side of San Antonio, a few miles from any one of four major military installations. He was not a senior officer living in base housing. He was a guy who'd been stationed in Frankfurt, found the right car at the right dealer, and wasn't leaving without it.                                 

The car spent most of its life in Texas after that, and I have some receipts that show Mr Campbell as the owner up until at least 1996. He was moved to Kansas for a couple years, but returned to Austin sometime around 1974. By the time I found it on Craigslist in August 2022, it had changed hands at least twice and been sitting in a garage in Austin for about ten years.

---

I was in high school in Salem, Oregon when I first saw a 2002. I remember walking around downtown and this car caught my eye. It was blue, upright, more glass than seemed reasonable for something that small. Clean and simple in a way that felt purposeful. I didn't know what it was at first, but I knew it was something. I found out, filed it somewhere in the back of my mind under "someday," and went on with my life.

Someday has a way of getting away from you. I spent twenty years in the bicycle industry, Eventually starting my own custom bicycle fabrication company. I slowly took on small motorcycle and car projects just to learn more about engines. The 2002 stayed filed where I'd put it. Then I got a little older and realized that someday doesn't wait forever. If I was going to have one, I needed to actually go get one.

The timing was not ideal. Classic car prices had been climbing, and the 2002 market had climbed with them. When this listing appeared in Austin in the summer of 2022, the asking price made me laugh. $20,000 for a car that, by the seller's own accounting, needed a frame rail, new door skin, cracked dash, seat work, and more. He wasn't hiding anything. Read the ad and you'll see a man who wanted the car to go to the right person more than he wanted top dollar:

*"I'm selling it because I had grandiose dreams of restoring it back to factory condition. It's been with me for about 10 years and my family and business have taken my focus and priorities for a long time now... I want it to go to someone who can and wants to complete my vision for this timeless and beautiful car."*

The ad also made a point of mentioning the Campagnolo wheels. Campagnolo was known for lightweight magnesium alloy wheels and was the OEM wheel supplier for Ferrari. Finding a set on a 2002 was a legitimate selling point.

![1973 BMW 2002 rear on the flatbed](/images/2002-02.jpg)

I called anyway. He told me the asking price was ambitious. We talked about the car. I asked a friend to come look at it with me, and when I saw it in person I was done negotiating too hard. It was blue. Of course it was blue.

I paid more than I should have and less than he asked. Part of the deal was that he got to keep the Campagnolos. I came back to pick the car up and it was sitting on its steel wheels with BMW hubcaps. Unpopular opinion, but I think the steelies actually suit the car far better. To my eyes, a 4 spoke wheel just doesn't feel right, it's too balanced, too square.

![BMW 2002 campy wheels](/images/2002-11.jpg)

![BMW 2002 in the driveway, steel wheels, Texas plate FKV-940](/images/2002-09.jpg)


After we loaded it on the trailer (with non working brakes! That was an adventure) I took it straight to a shop here in Austin where some of the best BMW mechanics in the city had landed after a local legend named Terry Sayther retired from working on 2002s. The frame rail situation made the decision easy: this wasn't optional work.

![BMW 2002 undercarriage showing the damaged frame rail](/images/2002-10.jpg)
*The swiss-cheese framerail needed immediate repair*

![BMW 2002 interior and dashboard](/images/2002-04.jpg)

They fixed the frame rail and sorted the brakes. The bill arrived with Ferrari-shop energy, which is what happens when your vintage BMW gets serviced at a place that also works on Ferraris. That cleaned out the budget. Everything else would have to wait, and would have to be done as cheaply as possible.

![BMW 2002 kidney grille detail](/images/2002-08.jpg)

---

It's been two years. The car still needs work. It doesn't drive like a dream yet. But I get it out to cars and coffee sometimes, and to the vintage Euro nights at Top Notch. It needs struts and the transmission doesn't shift into 2nd without knowing the handshake. I'll get to it. I've had it for two years and counting.

Still blue. There's more to do. I'll be writing about it as it happens.
